Why This Exists

A clinical framework for an unclinical conversation.

AI vendors test their models in development. They do not assess how those models will fail in your hospital. The AI safety conversation, as it is currently being held, lives in technical vocabulary that does not translate into the discipline your quality department already runs on.

SAFE AI brings the two methodologies your quality, risk, and patient safety teams already use — root cause analysis and failure mode and effects analysis — to the AI systems being deployed into clinical workflows. The output is an inventory of identified failure modes, scored on severity, occurrence, and detection, tied to mitigation actions your existing governance structure can execute.

What separates this from a standard quality exercise is where the failure mode catalog begins. Not from a blank sheet, and not from vendor documentation. It begins from the Cure8 Signal Registry — a continuously curated body of peer-reviewed clinical AI literature maintained by research agents that actively scan publications, extract emerging patterns of failure and success, and build an evidence base that exists before your engagement starts. By the time we assess your environment, we already know what the literature shows about how these systems break.

The discipline is not new. The evidence foundation is.

The Framework

Two methodologies, adapted.

SAFE AI runs two parallel analyses on every in-scope AI system.

RCA for AI Failures

When a model degrades in production, the failure rarely originates in the model itself. SAFE AI applies the same root cause discipline used after a sentinel event to AI failures.

We map the model's training data lineage, feature pipeline, deployment context, and update history, then trace observed degradation back to a contributing or causal factor.

AI-specific causal categories: training data drift, feature drift, deployment context mismatch, silent retraining, prompt injection, unmonitored vendor updates.

FMEA for AI Failure Modes

Before failure happens, SAFE AI catalogs how it could — starting from an evidence foundation no engagement has to build from scratch. The Cure8 Signal Registry provides that foundation: a continuously curated body of peer-reviewed clinical AI literature, maintained by research agents that actively scan publications, extract emerging patterns of failure and success, and surface what the evidence actually shows about how these systems break and where they hold.

When we engage with your environment, that literature baseline is already in place. We layer your specific deployment context on top — the system, your patient population, existing controls, and monitoring infrastructure — and produce a failure mode catalog shaped to what is actually running in your care setting, not a generic checklist built from first principles.

Each mode is then scored on severity, occurrence, and detection to produce a Risk Priority Number. Modes are ranked and mitigation actions are assigned against a threshold your team sets.

The Discipline Behind It

Why patient safety frameworks work on AI.

The methodology used to investigate medication errors, surgical complications, and diagnostic delays applies directly to model failures. The failure-and-recovery dynamics are structurally similar: a system performs as expected most of the time, fails under conditions that were not anticipated, and the question is whether the organization can detect, contain, and learn from the failure.

The vocabulary your quality department already speaks — root cause, contributing factor, severity, detection, RPN, mitigation, escalation — is the vocabulary SAFE AI uses. The framework slots into the governance structures you already have, not new ones you have to build.

The evidence base behind it is built the same way. Research agents continuously monitor the peer-reviewed clinical AI literature, identifying both failure patterns and success conditions across deployment contexts. That ongoing curation means the failure mode catalog we bring to your environment reflects what is actually emerging in the field — not a fixed list assembled at framework inception and never revisited.

That is the point. Clinical AI does not need a new safety language, and it does not need a theoretical failure taxonomy. It needs the existing discipline applied with current evidence behind it.
