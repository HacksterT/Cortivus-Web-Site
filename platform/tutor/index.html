Coffee Roasting Fundamentals

Phase 1: Foundation - Core Stages and Terminology

Welcome to Phase 1 of your Coffee Roasting syllabus. We'll break down the four core roasting stages, key terms like ROR and cracks, and the chemistry shift from endothermic to exothermic. By the end, you'll spot these in any roast, setting you up for hands-on mastery.

1.1 Roasting Stages Overview

Picture this: you're staring at a drum roaster loaded with green Ethiopian beans. They start grassy and wet, but over 10-15 minutes, they transform through four distinct stages into aromatic gold.

Drying Phase

25-160°C (77-320°F) | 4-6 minutes

Beans lose 10-12% moisture down to 2-5%. It's all endothermic—beans suck up heat like a sponge, so Rate of Rise (ROR) slows. Smells? Grassy and hay-like.

Browning/Maillard Phase

160-195°C (320-383°F) | 3-5 minutes

Aromas shift from grass to toast and bread as Maillard reactions kick in. ROR picks up as moisture's gone. This builds the base flavors.

Development Phase

195-220°C (383-428°F) | 1-3 minutes

Post-First Crack: beans expand, oils form—pure magic, but easy to overdo. Ends at Second Crack or your quench call.

Cooling/Quenching

Drop to <50°C (122°F) | 2-4 minutes

Fans or water cool beans fast to lock in flavors—no further cooking!

Key Takeaways
  • Drying: Heat-absorbing moisture loss, grassy aromas
  • Maillard: Flavor-building browning, bread smells
  • Development: Post-crack flavor peak, time it tight
  • Cooling: Quench fast to halt reactions

1.2 Key Terminology: ROR, Cracks, and Reactions

Rate of Rise (ROR)

Bean temp increase per minute (°F/min or °C/min). Starts low in drying, peaks mid-Maillard, dips if stalling. Target: smooth downward curve post-charge for even roast.

First Crack

~166-177°C (330-350°F) at ~8-10 min. Exothermic explosion: gases from Maillard pressure beans to audibly snap like popcorn. Marks development start.

Second Crack

~205-215°C (401-419°F). Finer, rolling crack; oils migrate, fruity notes emerge. Risky zone—overdo for ashiness.

Maillard Reaction

Amino acids + sugars create nutty, bready flavors. Caramelization breaks down sugars post-crack for sweetness.

1.3 Endothermic to Exothermic Chemistry

Understanding why beans shift from heat-hungry to heat-releasing is key to avoiding stalls or scorches.

Endothermic (Early)

Beans absorb heat for evaporation and reactions—ROR slows as energy fights physics. Beans swell 50-100%, volume doubles.

Exothermic (Post-FC)

Gases and oils from breakdowns release heat, spiking ROR. Beans crack audibly—expansion hits 13-15% weight loss.

Activity: Map a Hypothetical Roast Timeline

Objective: Build a mental timeline for a 12-minute drum roast of green Ethiopian beans, labeling stages, ROR shifts, aromas, cracks, and quench.

  1. Draw X-axis (0-15 min), Y-axis (bean temp 0-220°C). Mark charge at 0 min (~180°C drum).
  2. Label Drying (0-5 min): End ~160°C, grassy to hay aromas, low ROR.
  3. Add Maillard (5-9 min): 160-195°C, bread aromas, rising ROR.
  4. Plot Development (9-11 min): FC at 9 min, ROR bump (exothermic).
  5. Mark Quench (11 min): Drop to cool. Sketch ROR curve.
